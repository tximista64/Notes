# What Should You Expect?
Do you want to start your career as a SOC analyst but don't know what’s waiting for you along this journey?
In this article, we will talk about the job descriptions of Security
Analyst and Incident Responders, the advantages and disadvantages of
being a SOC analyst, and finally we will check the average cybersecurity
salaries general.
### What Does the Security Analyst (Tier 1) Do?
The main task of the Tier 1 SOC analyst is monitoring. Tier 1 analyst
performs daily monitoring routines during working hours and reviews
alarms that the security tools generate. Analyst provides feedback to
the relevant team/engineer about the configurations of the security
monitoring tools. The cybersecurity analyst is the one who responds to
attacks first. He/she determines the severity of the alarms, and decides
whether there is attack traffic.He/she then prioritizes them according
to severity levels. Tier 1 analysts escalate the alarms for further
investigations if they are not able to obtain healthy results after
their investigations. The SOC environment works 24/7 in most
organizations to monitor all the IT equipments and network devices. Tier
1 Analysts implement the following tasks:
- Monitoring the network using various security products/tools,
- Tracking and investigating triggered alerts,
- Providing feedback on security vulnerabilities/gaps they detected,
- Providing feedback to reduce the false positive rate of the rules,
- Implementing blocks of the malicious IOCs on the security products to prevent future incidents,
- Following the appropriate procedures and escalating cases that need further investigation.
### Required Skills
- Continuous monitoring,
- Ability to work under stress,
- Technical knowledge of Network and Security principles,
- Adapting to shift work,
- Communication and coordination,
- Ability to use time efficiently,
- Error control check
### What Does Incident Responder (Tier 2) Do?
The Tier 2 SOC analysts are responsible for the detailed
investigation of the systems that raise alerts and those that are
escalated by the Tier 1 SOC analysts. Tier 2 analyst reviews and
investigate the technical details in order to understand the incident
and make a conclusion out of it. Tier 2 SOC analysts are more technical
the Tier 1 SOC analysts and they are mostly called Incident Responders.
A Tier 2 SOC Analyst is more experienced and technical than the Tier 1
analyst and uses both analytical and holistic perspectives when
investigating the case. Tier 2 analysts try to determine whether an
anomaly occurs and critical systems and data are affected under the
influence of an attack by reconciling the technical details obtained
from various sources of information. Tier 2 analysts also determine the
remediation plan by making recommendations for improvement as well as
create a recovery plan after the incident. They may develop new
analytical methods when detecting threats. Regardless of their seniority
level, all the SOC analysts compete against time and try to find the
indicators formed on the systems of threats as soon as possible.
### Required Skills
- Analytical and holistic thinking,
- Ability to work under stress, and short deadlines for timely critical cases,
- Excellent communication skills,
- Ability to reconcile technical details accurately and reporting skills,
- Incident response and management,
- Technical knowledge of Network and Security principles,
- Ability to follow processes, procedures, and rules correctly,
- Discipline.
### Advantages and Disadvantages of Being a SOC Analyst
Being a SOC analyst has some advantages and disadvantages. Although
these may vary from organization to organization, we can summarize them
as below:
### Advantages:
- Above average compensation opportunities,
- Ability to work remotely,
- Opportunity to constantly improve and grow,
- Being a part of the new growing blue team industry.
### Disadvantages:
- Working in the stressful environments,
- Working against time and work on short deadlines,
- 24/7 work environments,
- Keeping up to date with developing technologies and threats,
- Long working hours,
- High burnout potential/risk,
- High job turnover rate
